How do CO₂ sensors work in demand-controlled ventilation?

Controls#CO2-sensor#demand-controlled-ventilation#ASHRAE-62.1#NDIR

CO₂ Sensors and Demand-Controlled Ventilation

Demand-controlled ventilation (DCV) using CO₂ sensors is a cornerstone strategy for reducing HVAC energy consumption while maintaining indoor air quality. By modulating outdoor air intake based on actual occupancy rather than fixed design assumptions, DCV can reduce heating and cooling of ventilation air by 20–40% in variably occupied spaces.

### How NDIR CO₂ Sensors Work

Non-Dispersive Infrared (NDIR) sensors are the standard technology for building automation CO₂ measurement. They operate by passing infrared light at a wavelength of 4.26 μm through an air sample in a measurement chamber. CO₂ molecules absorb energy at this specific wavelength, and the degree of absorption — measured by a photodetector — is proportional to the CO₂ concentration. A reference channel at a nearby non-absorbing wavelength compensates for sensor ageing, dust accumulation, and temperature drift.

Modern NDIR sensors achieve ±30–50 ppm accuracy with a measurement range of 0–2,000 ppm (standard) or 0–5,000 ppm (extended range for specialised applications). Dual-beam designs with Automatic Baseline Calibration (ABC) logic periodically reset the sensor's baseline to the lowest CO₂ reading observed over a 7–14 day rolling window, assuming the space reaches outdoor ambient levels (400–450 ppm) at some point during each week. Without ABC, sensor drift of 20–30 ppm per year is typical and can accumulate to significant error within 3–5 years.

### The DCV Control Logic

The BAS controller calculates the required outdoor air based on the differential between zone CO₂ concentration and outdoor ambient concentration. Per ASHRAE 62.1, DCV is permitted in spaces where the per-person outdoor air rate (Rp) dominates the ventilation calculation — spaces like conference rooms, auditoriums, and open offices where occupancy varies significantly. The typical DCV setpoint is an indoor concentration 350–550 ppm above outdoor ambient (approximately 1,000–1,100 ppm absolute), corresponding to the Rp per-person ventilation rate.

### Sensor Placement — The Most Common Failure Point

Incorrect sensor placement undermines DCV effectiveness more than any other factor. Sensors must be mounted at breathing height (1.2–1.8 m AFF), on an interior wall, at least 1.5 m from doors, operable windows, and supply air diffusers. A sensor near a door reads towards outdoor ambient regardless of space occupancy. A sensor in the direct path of a supply diffuser reads diluted air that does not represent the breathing zone. A sensor in direct sunlight reads artificially high as the chamber heats and the NDIR measurement shifts.

For large open-plan spaces, multiple sensors may be required — typically one per 250–500 m² or one per distinct occupancy zone. Sensors should be connected to the nearest VAV controller or directly to the AHU-level BAS controller, and the highest reading among multiple sensors should drive the outdoor air damper position (worst-case control strategy).
