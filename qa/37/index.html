How do I choose between a globe valve and a ball valve for HVAC control?

Hydronic#globe-valve#ball-valve#control-valve#characterized

Globe Valve vs Characterised Ball Valve for HVAC Control

The choice between globe and ball valves for modulating HVAC control has shifted significantly over the past decade as characterised ball valve technology has matured. Understanding the trade-offs in flow characteristic, pressure drop, close-off capability, and lifecycle cost is essential for modern hydronic system design.

### Flow Characteristic and Rangeability

Globe valves achieve their characterised flow (typically equal-percentage) through a contoured plug that varies the annular flow area as the stem lifts. This provides excellent rangeability — typically 50:1 — meaning the valve can control accurately from 2% to 100% of rated flow. The Cv vs. stem travel relationship is well-documented and predictable, which is why globe valves have been the default choice for decades.

Characterised ball valves use a V-notch or segmented ball that creates a similar equal-percentage characteristic through the rotation of the ball. The V-port shape is precision-machined to produce a specific flow characteristic, and modern characterised ball valves achieve comparable rangeability (50:1 or better) with the added benefit of a full-port design that dramatically reduces pressure drop.

### Pressure Drop and Pump Energy

The most significant advantage of characterised ball valves is their lower pressure drop when fully open. A globe valve inherently restricts flow — even when fully open, the tortuous flow path through the seat and around the plug creates several PSI of pressure drop. A full-port ball valve, by contrast, has a straight-through flow path with almost no obstruction when fully open. At design flow, this can translate to 3–5 PSI less pressure drop per valve, which across dozens of valves in a typical building can reduce pump head requirements by 20–30% and deliver substantial pumping energy savings over the system lifecycle.

### Close-Off and Leakage

Ball valves with characterised trim achieve significantly higher close-off pressures than equivalent-sized globe valves because the ball is supported on both sides by seats. They can also achieve tighter shutoff — ANSI Class IV or VI for soft-seated designs — compared to the metal-to-metal seats common in globe valves which typically achieve Class II or III. For isolation duty or applications requiring zero leakage during unoccupied periods, characterised ball valves are superior.

### When Globe Valves Still Win

Globe valves remain the preferred choice for steam applications because their metal seat design tolerates the higher temperatures (up to 400°F+) and thermal cycling that can degrade soft-seated ball valves. They are also preferred for very small flow rates (Cv < 1.0) where the precision machining required for a small V-port ball becomes cost-prohibitive, and for applications requiring ANSI Class VI shutoff in high-temperature service where soft-seated ball valves cannot meet the temperature rating.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • ✓Characterised ball valves offer 3–5 PSI lower pressure drop per valve compared to globe valves, reducing pump head and delivering lifecycle energy savings
  • ✓Both valve types achieve equal-percentage characteristics with 50:1 rangeability — the flow control quality is comparable when properly sized
  • ✓Ball valves outperform globe valves on close-off pressure and shutoff leakage (Class IV–VI vs Class II–III)
  • ✓Globe valves remain preferred for steam applications and very small flow rates (Cv < 1.0) where high temperatures degrade soft-seated ball valves
  • ✓For Australian chilled water and heating hot water systems, characterised ball PICVs are now the industry standard for new construction
