How do I select the appropriate damper actuator control signal — on/off, floating, or modulating?

Actuators#control-signal#modulating#floating#on-off

Selecting Damper Actuator Control Signals — On/Off, Floating, or Modulating

The control signal type is one of the first decisions when specifying a damper actuator, and it directly determines control precision, wiring complexity, and integration capability with the building automation system.

### On/Off (2-Position) Actuators

Two-position actuators are the simplest — they drive to fully open (or fully closed) when power is applied and spring-return to the opposite position (or stay in place for non-spring-return) when power is removed. They typically use 24 VAC/VDC or 120 VAC power and require only two power conductors plus optional end-switch wiring for position feedback.

Applications are limited to open/close functions: garage exhaust fans, toilet exhaust isolation, smoke damper actuation, and equipment isolation. They are unsuitable for any application requiring modulation — economiser control, VAV boxes, face and bypass dampers — because they cannot achieve intermediate positions.

### Floating (3-Point) Actuators

Floating actuators accept two digital pulse signals — one to drive open, one to drive close — from a controller with triac or relay outputs. With both signals off, the actuator holds position. A typical runtime is 90–150 seconds from fully closed to fully open, and the controller tracks inferred position by counting the accumulated pulse time.

Floating control is used when the BAS controller lacks an analogue output (0–10V or 4–20mA) but needs modulating functionality. The controller must periodically drive the actuator to a known reference position (typically fully closed) to recalibrate its inferred position — without this, position error accumulates over time from short pulses that are too brief to overcome stiction.

While floating actuators are less expensive than true modulating types and require simpler controller outputs, they have two significant drawbacks: position accuracy degrades without regular recalibration, and the triac-output controllers needed to drive them are being phased out in favour of universal I/O modules with analogue outputs.

### Modulating (Analogue) Actuators

Modulating actuators accept a 0–10 VDC or 4–20 mA analogue signal and position the damper proportionally across the full 0–100% range. A 2–10 VDC signal with 0–2 VDC reserved for fault indication is the BAS industry standard. The actuator provides an analogue position feedback signal (typically 2–10 VDC) that the controller can read to verify actual position.

These actuators are required for economiser dampers, VAV boxes (though many now use integrated DDC controllers with onboard actuator drives), face and bypass dampers, and any application where precise, repeatable positioning is needed for stable temperature or pressure control.

### The BACnet/Modbus Advantage

The latest generation of modulating actuators — such as Belimo's MP bus-enabled and BACnet MS/TP models — communicate digitally with the BAS, reporting not just position but runtime hours, cycle count, torque load, and diagnostic data. This enables automated fault detection — the BMS can flag an actuator approaching its rated cycle life or one drawing abnormal current indicating impending failure — well before the damper fails to control.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • ✓On/off actuators suit simple isolation and safety functions; floating actuators enable basic modulation from digital controller outputs.
  • ✓Modulating 0–10V actuators provide true proportional positioning and are the minimum standard for economisers and AHU mixing dampers.
  • ✓Floating actuators require periodic recalibration to correct accumulated position error — typically by driving fully closed on a schedule.
  • ✓BACnet MS/TP modulating actuators report runtime, cycle count, and torque diagnostics for automated fault detection — a significant operational advantage.
  • ✓The 2–10 VDC signal range with 0–2 VDC reserved for fault indication is the BAS industry standard for analogue actuator control.
