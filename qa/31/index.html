How do I size a heating hot water control valve?

Hydronic#heating#control-valve#Cv#hot-water

Sizing Heating Hot Water Control Valves

Heating hot water (HHW) control valve sizing follows the same fundamental formula as chilled water — Cv = Q × √(SG/ΔP) — but the practical application differs significantly due to higher operating temperatures, different system pressures, and the dominance of low-load operation.

### The Cv Calculation for Heating

For heating coils, the design flow rate Q is determined by the heating load at design outdoor air temperature. With typical HHW supply temperatures of 80–95°C and return temperatures 10–20°C lower, the mass flow required for a given heating load is lower than for chilled water (where the available ΔT is only 5–7°C). A coil that requires 0.5 L/s of chilled water might need only 0.15–0.25 L/s of heating water for the same duty.

### Valve Authority in Heating Systems

Heating valves must contend with higher system pressures than their chilled water counterparts. HHW distribution systems typically operate at 40–60 PSI differential at the pump, compared to 25–40 PSI for chilled water, because the higher temperatures require higher system pressures to prevent flashing and cavitation. This means the valve must be selected for close-off against higher pressures, and potentially requires a smaller valve (higher ΔP across the valve) to achieve good authority.

The target valve authority for heating applications is 0.3–0.5, consistent with general HVAC practice, but achieving this can be challenging because the coil itself has a low pressure drop at the reduced heating flow rates. A coil with 10 kPa pressure drop at cooling flow may have only 1–2 kPa at heating flow — the control valve must therefore take a larger share of the available branch pressure drop.

### Sizing for Low-Load Operation

The critical sizing challenge for HHW valves is that they operate at low loads for the vast majority of their service life. A perimeter heating coil may see design load for fewer than 100 hours per year. For the remaining 8,600+ hours, it operates at 10–30% of design flow. In this range, an oversized valve operates in the bottom 10% of its stroke where the installed gain is highest and stable control is nearly impossible.

The solution is to size the valve conservatively — the fully open Cv should be no more than 1.3× the calculated design Cv, and the valve should be capable of achieving design flow at no more than 70–80% open. This ensures the valve operates in its characterised range (20–80% open) at typical part-load conditions.

### Material and Temperature Considerations

Valve bodies and seats for HHW service must be continuously rated for 95°C minimum (110°C for high-temperature systems). EPDM seats are standard for temperatures up to 120°C, while PTFE or metal seats are required above this. Actuators mounted on HHW valves should include a thermal neck or standoff to prevent heat conduction from the valve body to the actuator electronics, which can cause premature failure.

Belimo's Characterised Control Valve (CCV) line works particularly well for HHW because the equal-percentage characteristic compensates for the logarithmic heat output curve of coils — as the valve opens, each increment of stem travel produces a proportionally larger increase in flow and heat output, linearising the control response.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • ✓HHW control valves should be sized conservatively — design Cv ≤ 1.3× the calculated requirement and valve ≤ 80% open at design flow.
  • ✓HHW valves operate at 10–30% of design flow for over 90% of annual hours; an oversized valve cannot control stably in this range.
  • ✓HHW systems operate at higher pressures than chilled water — verify close-off pressure against the higher pump deadhead and apply 1.5× safety factor.
  • ✓Valve bodies and seats must be continuously rated for 95°C minimum; actuators require thermal necks to prevent heat damage to electronics.
  • ✓Equal-percentage characterised valves (Belimo CCV or equivalent) provide the best control linearity for heating coils at typical part-load conditions.
