What is the difference between 24V AC and 24V DC actuator power?

Actuators#power-supply#24V#AC-DC#actuator

24V AC vs 24V DC Actuator Power — A Practical Guide

The choice between 24V AC and 24V DC actuator power supplies is increasingly important as the industry transitions from traditional transformer-based power to DC-powered intelligent actuators with electronic fail-safe capabilities.

### The 24V AC Legacy

For decades, 24V AC was the de facto standard for HVAC actuators. Transformers were simple, reliable, and inexpensive — a 75 VA Class 2 transformer could power multiple actuators and controllers with inherent galvanic isolation. The AC waveform also enabled early 2–10 VDC position feedback circuits without separate DC power supplies; a simple bridge rectifier and filter capacitor in the actuator derived the DC control voltage from the AC supply.

However, 24V AC actuators have inherent limitations. Spring-return designs must continuously energise a solenoid or motor against the spring — drawing 3–7 VA holding current even when the actuator is stationary. This holding energy is wasted as heat. The spring itself adds mechanical complexity, weight, and cost, and is a wear item with a rated cycle life (typically 60,000 full cycles for standard actuators).

### The 24V DC Advantage

Modern 24V DC actuators use brushless DC (BLDC) motors that are inherently more efficient than the shaded-pole or synchronous AC motors used in 24V AC designs. BLDC motors provide higher starting torque, better speed control, and consume power only when moving — the holding current at the setpoint is essentially zero. This alone can reduce actuator energy consumption by 60–80% compared to continuously energised spring-return AC actuators.

The most significant innovation enabled by 24V DC is capacitive fail-safe technology. Instead of a mechanical spring, capacitors (Belimo SuperCap technology) store enough energy during normal operation to drive the actuator to its fail-safe position on power loss. This eliminates the mechanical spring entirely — reducing weight, complexity, and wear points while providing the same safety function. The capacitors charge in 30–60 seconds after power-up and retain their charge for days, ensuring fail-safe operation even during intermittent power interruptions.

### The Retrofit Trap

A critical caution for retrofit projects: 24V AC actuators generally cannot be powered from a 24V DC supply. The internal circuitry — particularly the zero-crossing detection used by triac-output synchronous motors for timing — expects an AC waveform. Applying DC to a 24V AC actuator will either cause no operation (the motor cannot start) or damage the internal electronics.

Conversely, many modern 24V DC actuators accept 24V AC as well — the internal switch-mode power supply rectifies and regulates whatever input it receives. However, this is not universal. Always check the manufacturer's specification sheet for the accepted supply voltage range before mixing power types. When in doubt, match the power supply to the actuator's nameplate rating.
