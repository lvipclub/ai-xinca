What are the key differences between BACnet MS/TP and BACnet/IP?

Controls#BACnet#MS/TP#BACnet-IP#network

Architectural Differences Between MS/TP and IP

BACnet MS/TP and BACnet/IP serve fundamentally different roles in a well-designed building automation architecture, and understanding where each belongs is critical to avoiding network congestion, integration failures, and commissioning delays.

### Physical Layer and Topology

BACnet MS/TP operates over RS-485, a differential serial bus that supports multi-drop wiring — meaning devices are daisy-chained along a single twisted-pair cable with no need for network switches. This makes it cost-effective for floor-level controllers where a single cable run can connect 30–50 VAV boxes or fan coil unit controllers in sequence. The trade-off is speed: MS/TP runs at 9,600–76,800 bps, which is adequate for individual terminal unit data (temperature, airflow, damper position) but insufficient for bulk data transfers like trend uploads or firmware downloads.

BACnet/IP uses standard Ethernet infrastructure operating at 100 Mbps to 1 Gbps. It employs a star topology where each device connects to a network switch, enabling parallel communication and much higher aggregate throughput. This is essential at the supervisory and enterprise level where a single BMS server may poll thousands of points across dozens of MS/TP trunks simultaneously.

### Broadcast Management and BBMD

A critical architectural consideration is that BACnet uses broadcast messages for device discovery (Who-Is/I-Am) and certain service requests. On a flat IP network, broadcasts are limited to the local subnet. For multi-subnet campus installations, BACnet/IP requires BBMD (BACnet Broadcast Management Device) servers — typically the supervisory controllers or routers — to forward broadcasts across IP subnets. Misconfiguration of BBMD tables is one of the most common causes of BACnet/IP integration failures, where devices on one subnet cannot discover devices on another.

### When to Use Each

Per industry best practice, MS/TP should be reserved for terminal-level controllers that communicate primarily with a single supervisory device. All controller-to-controller peer communication, operator workstation traffic, and inter-building integration should use BACnet/IP. A common design pattern is to install BACnet routers that bridge 3–6 MS/TP trunks onto a single BACnet/IP backbone, with each trunk serving 30–40 devices on one floor or wing of the building.

### Security Considerations

Neither BACnet MS/TP nor standard BACnet/IP provides native encryption or authentication. BACnet/SC (Secure Connect), introduced in ASHRAE 135-2020, adds TLS 1.3 encryption and certificate-based device authentication to BACnet/IP — a capability that MS/TP cannot match due to bandwidth limitations. For projects requiring cybersecurity compliance (e.g., Australian Government ISM guidelines), BACnet/SC over IP should be specified for all backbone and supervisory communications, with MS/TP isolated to physically secure field-level networks.
