What is supply air temperature reset and how is it implemented?

Air-side#SAT-reset#energy-efficiency#ASHRAE-90.1#ASHRAE-36

Supply Air Temperature Reset — Strategy and Implementation

Supply air temperature (SAT) reset is one of the most impactful energy conservation measures available in VAV air handling systems, yet it remains under-implemented in many existing buildings. The concept is straightforward: when all zones are satisfied and dampers are near minimum position, the AHU is over-cooling the supply air, and the SAT setpoint can be raised to save energy.

### The Trim-and-Respond Mechanism

ASHRAE Guideline 36 specifies a trim-and-respond algorithm for SAT reset that mirrors the approach used for duct static pressure. The BMS controller polls all zone temperature controllers every 2–5 minutes. If every zone indicates it is receiving adequate cooling (typically defined as the zone temperature being at or below setpoint and the VAV damper at minimum position), the SAT setpoint is trimmed upward by 0.1–0.2°C. If any zone's damper exceeds a threshold (typically 85–95% open) or the zone temperature rises above setpoint, the algorithm responds by trimming the SAT setpoint back down.

### Energy Impact

Raising the SAT from the design condition (typically 12–13°C) to 16–18°C during part-load periods reduces the load on the cooling coil and chiller. For every 1°C increase in SAT, the cooling coil load decreases by approximately 5–8% depending on outdoor air fraction. More importantly, higher SAT enables more hours of air-side economiser operation — when outdoor air is below the SAT setpoint, the economiser can provide 100% of the cooling with zero mechanical cooling energy. In temperate Australian climates (e.g., Melbourne, Adelaide, Canberra), this can translate to hundreds of additional economiser hours annually.

### Upper Limit Constraints

The SAT reset cannot continue indefinitely. The practical upper limit is determined by the zone with the highest sensible cooling load (often perimeter zones on a hot day) and by dehumidification requirements. In humid climates or during humid weather, the SAT must remain low enough to condense moisture from the outdoor air — typically below the apparatus dew point of 12–13°C. If the SAT rises above the dew point of the supply airstream, the coil provides sensible cooling only, and space humidity will rise. ASHRAE Guideline 36 recommends capping the SAT reset at 18–20°C maximum unless humidity sensors confirm acceptable space conditions.

### Australian Implementation Considerations

For Australian projects, SAT reset must be coordinated with the NCC Section J energy efficiency requirements. Many Australian BMS contractors implement a simplified version of Guideline 36 SAT reset using rolling-average zone demand calculation across the JCI N2 or BACnet network, with typical upper limits of 18°C for office applications and 16°C for spaces with high latent loads such as theatres or gymnasiums.

  • ✓SAT reset raises the supply air temperature setpoint when all zones indicate adequate cooling, reducing chiller energy by 5–8% per degree Celsius of reset
  • ✓ASHRAE Guideline 36 trim-and-respond implementation polls zone demand every 3–5 minutes and adjusts the SAT setpoint in 0.1–0.15°C increments
