How do I commission a VAV airflow sensor?

Air-side#VAV#commissioning#airflow#sensor-calibration

Commissioning VAV Airflow Sensors

Accurate airflow measurement is the foundation of VAV system control — every ventilation calculation, every damper position, and every energy model depends on the VAV controller knowing how much air it is delivering. Commissioning the airflow sensor correctly is therefore one of the most important tasks in the entire building commissioning process.

### Understanding the K-Factor

VAV airflow sensors are typically pitot-tube arrays or velocity pressure sensors mounted in the box inlet collar. They measure velocity pressure (the difference between total and static pressure) at multiple points and the controller converts this to airflow using a k-factor: Airflow = k × √(ΔP × Area). The k-factor is specific to the box size, inlet configuration, and sensor type — changing any of these invalidates the factory-calibrated value. A k-factor error of even 10% translates to a 10% airflow error, which can mean non-compliance with ASHRAE 62.1 minimum ventilation requirements.

### The Calibration Procedure

The correct procedure per ASHRAE Guideline 36 and ASHRAE Standard 111 involves measuring actual airflow at the zone diffuser(s) using a calibrated flow hood (Shortridge, Alnor, or TSI) while the VAV controller is commanded to specific damper positions. The process must verify at minimum four operating points:

1. **Maximum cooling airflow** — fully open damper at design airflow

2. **Minimum cooling airflow** — the lowest airflow in cooling mode (typically 20–30% of maximum) 3. **Heating airflow** — if the box is fan-powered or has reheat 4. **At least one intermediate point** — typically 50–60% of maximum

At each point, the flow hood measurement and controller reading should agree within ±5%. If they diverge, the k-factor is adjusted and the measurements repeated. This is tedious work but the alternative — accepting inaccurate readings — undermines every control sequence downstream.

### Common Pitfalls

Leaking or kinked pressure tubing between the pickup array and the controller's DP transducer is the most common cause of inaccurate readings. Even a pinhole leak can introduce errors of 20–30%. Water traps in the tubing — caused by condensation in cold supply airstreams — can completely block the pressure signal. The pickup itself must be verified to be centred in the inlet collar and oriented correctly; many are installed rotated or pushed to one side during rough-in and never corrected.

Modern DDC VAV controllers from JCI (FX-PCV series) and Siemens (ATEC series) include auto-zero and auto-calibration routines that simplify the process, but these should still be verified against a flow hood — the auto-calibration can compensate for sensor drift but cannot correct for a fundamentally mis-installed pickup.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • ✓VAV airflow accuracy depends entirely on the k-factor — a 10% k-factor error produces a 10% airflow error and potential ASHRAE 62.1 non-compliance.
  • ✓Calibrate at minimum four points: maximum cooling, minimum cooling, heating airflow (if applicable), and at least one intermediate point.
  • ✓The most common cause of airflow error is leaking or kinked pressure tubing — inspect physically before any electronic calibration.
  • ✓Acceptance criterion is ±5% agreement between the flow hood reference and controller reading at all operating points.
  • ✓Document as-found and as-left values for every point in the commissioning report — this is a code-required deliverable.
