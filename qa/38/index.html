Where should humidity sensors be placed in an AHU?

Controls#humidity#sensor-placement#AHU#RH

Humidity Sensor Placement in Air Handling Units

Humidity measurement is inherently more challenging than temperature measurement because Relative Humidity (RH) sensors are sensitive to contamination, condensation, and local microclimates that may not represent the bulk airstream. Strategic placement at each measurement location in the AHU is critical for reliable dew point control and energy-efficient operation.

### Return Air Humidity Sensor

The return air sensor provides the primary indication of average space humidity. It should be located in the return air duct upstream of any mixing with outdoor air — ideally in a straight duct section where flow is well-developed. The sensor probe must extend into the centre of the duct where air is well-mixed, not in a stagnant corner where stratification can produce readings 5–10% RH different from the bulk airstream. For return air plenums (common in ceiling return systems), an averaging sensor array spanning the plenum width provides better representation than a single point sensor.

### Outdoor Air Humidity Sensor

Outdoor humidity sensing is critical for economiser enthalpy changeover calculations and for determining whether mechanical dehumidification is required. The sensor must be protected from direct solar radiation, rain, and wind-driven contamination. A naturally aspirated or fan-aspirated radiation shield — the same type used for outdoor temperature sensors — is standard. Mounting on a north-facing wall (in the Southern Hemisphere) minimises solar radiation errors that can cause RH readings to drift by 5–15% during peak sun hours. The sensor must also be rated for the full outdoor temperature range (-20°C to 50°C for most Australian climate zones) and protected against condensation freezing on the sensing element.

### Supply Air Humidity Sensor

Supply air humidity monitoring is used for dew point control strategies where the supply air dew point is the controlled variable rather than space RH. The sensor must be mounted downstream of the cooling coil and supply fan — at least 3–4 duct diameters from the coil discharge — to avoid the saturated air or water droplet carryover that occurs immediately after the coil. Even with adequate distance, a sintered filter cap on the sensor probe is recommended to protect against occasional mist. In systems with downstream heating coils or terminal reheat, note that supply duct RH readings will be lower than coil-leaving conditions due to sensible heating (RH decreases as temperature increases at constant moisture content).

### Sensor Technology and Maintenance

Capacitive RH sensors are the industry standard, offering ±2–3% RH accuracy in the 20–80% RH range. However, this accuracy degrades significantly if the sensor is exposed to condensing conditions — the water film on the polymer dielectric causes hysteresis and permanent calibration drift. Annual calibration verification against a traceable reference standard (e.g., saturated salt solution calibration chambers) is essential for any sensor used in a control sequence.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • ✓Return air humidity sensors must be in the duct centre or use averaging arrays — plenum stratification can cause 5–10% RH errors
  • ✓Outdoor humidity sensors require aspirated radiation shields on north-facing walls (Southern Hemisphere) to avoid solar-induced 5–15% RH drift

  • ✓Capacitive RH sensors exposed to condensation suffer permanent calibration drift — use sintered filter caps and annual calibration verification
  • ✓For dew point control strategies, the supply air humidity sensor is the primary control input and must be commissioned with ±2% RH accuracy
