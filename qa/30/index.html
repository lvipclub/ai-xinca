What is actuator close-off pressure and why is it critical?

Actuators#close-off#actuator#pressure#sizing

Actuator Close-Off Pressure — Why It Is Critical

Close-off pressure is arguably the most important single specification on an actuator datasheet, yet it is frequently overlooked during selection and leads to some of the most expensive field failures in HVAC systems. Understanding what close-off pressure means, how to calculate the requirement, and why safety factors are essential can prevent catastrophic system failures.

### Definition and Physics

Close-off pressure is the maximum differential pressure against which an actuator can fully seat its damper or valve and hold it sealed. If the system pressure exceeds the actuator's close-off rating, the damper blades will lift off their seals (or the valve plug will lift off its seat), resulting in uncontrolled leakage. In the worst case — a large outdoor air damper blowing open in freezing weather — the result is a ruptured heating coil, thousands of dollars in damage, and building downtime.

The close-off requirement is calculated differently for dampers and control valves:

- **Dampers**: Close-off pressure = Maximum fan static pressure at shutoff + wind pressure (for outdoor dampers) + stack effect (for high-rise shafts). A variable-speed fan at full speed with all VAV boxes closed produces its maximum shutoff pressure — typically 1.3–1.5× the design operating static pressure.

  • **Control valves**: Close-off pressure = Pump deadhead pressure at the valve location minus the pressure drop across the downstream piping. In tall buildings, the hydrostatic head adds significantly — a valve in the basement of a 30-storey building may see 10–15 bar of static pressure before the pump even starts.

    ### The Safety Factor

    A safety factor of 1.5× over the calculated close-off requirement is standard practice. This accounts for:

    - **Pump or fan performance variation**: Actual shutoff pressure may exceed the published curve, especially with aged impellers or fans.

  • **System pressure transients**: Water hammer in hydronic systems can produce momentary pressures 2–3× the steady-state value.
  • **Actuator ageing**: Spring-return actuators lose 5–10% of their rated torque over 60,000+ cycles as spring fatigue sets in.
  • **Seal compression set**: Aged damper seals require higher torque to achieve the same seal compression.

    ### Using Manufacturer Close-Off Tables

    Major actuator manufacturers publish detailed close-off tables that map actuator torque to maximum damper area at various differential pressures. A Belimo 20 N·m actuator, for example, can close a 1.5 m² opposed-blade damper against 2,000 Pa (8 in. w.g.), but only a 1.0 m² damper at 3,000 Pa (12 in. w.g.). These tables are specific to the damper blade type (opposed vs parallel), seal type (EPDM, silicone, or metal), and manufacturer — always use the table from the damper manufacturer, not the actuator manufacturer, as the final authority.

    ### Common Field Failures

    The most common close-off failure is specifying an actuator based on the design operating pressure rather than the maximum shutoff pressure. In a VAV system, the duct static pressure at the AHU discharge during design operation may be 750 Pa — but the fan shutoff pressure at full speed is 1,250 Pa. An actuator sized for 750 Pa close-off will blow open when the system transitions to unoccupied mode and the VAV boxes close.

    🔑 Key Takeaways

    • ✓Close-off pressure is the maximum differential pressure at which the actuator can hold the damper or valve fully sealed — exceeding it causes uncontrolled leakage or blow-open.
    • ✓Always calculate close-off based on maximum shutoff conditions (fan at full speed, pump at deadhead), not design operating conditions.
    • ✓Apply a 1.5× safety factor to cover pump/fan variation, pressure transients, actuator ageing, and seal compression set.
    • ✓Wind loading (+125–250 Pa for outdoor dampers) and stack effect (+25 Pa per floor in high-rise) are additive to the fan shutoff requirement.
    • ✓Use the close-off table from the damper or valve manufacturer — not the actuator manufacturer — as the final selection authority.
