What types of damper seals are available and when should I use each?

Air-side#damper#seals#leakage#AMCA

Damper Seal Types and Selection

Damper seals are the critical interface between the blade edge and the frame that determines leakage performance, operational torque, and long-term durability. Selecting the wrong seal material for the application can lead to excessive leakage, actuator overload, or premature seal failure.

### Compression-Type Edge Seals

The workhorse of commercial HVAC dampers is the EPDM (ethylene propylene diene monomer) rubber bulb seal. This compression-type seal is extruded as a hollow profile that compresses against the adjacent blade or frame when the damper closes, providing a flexible, self-compensating seal. EPDM performs well up to 95°C continuous (110°C intermittent) and resists ozone, UV, and most common HVAC environmental conditions. At 250 Pa (1 in. w.g.) test pressure, EPDM-sealed dampers typically achieve leakage rates of 40–200 L/s/m² (8–40 CFM/ft²), qualifying for AMCA Class 1 or 1A.

**Silicone rubber seals** extend the temperature range to 175°C continuous and are specified when EPDM off-gassing is a concern — such as in laboratory exhaust or healthcare applications where volatile organic compounds from EPDM could contaminate supply air. Silicone seals are more expensive (typically 30–50% premium) and have lower tear strength than EPDM, so they require more careful handling during installation.

### Metal Seals for High-Temperature and Smoke Control

**Stainless steel jamb seals** (flexible 304 or 316 stainless strips) are required for smoke control dampers tested to UL 555S. These seals maintain integrity at 175°C for minimum 30 minutes (or higher for specific ratings) where elastomeric seals would degrade or ignite. The metal-to-metal contact provides a leakage Class I rating (<50 L/s/m² at 1 kPa or <8 CFM/ft² at 4 in. w.g.) when properly installed. The trade-off is higher actuator torque — metal seals have no compressibility and generate significantly more friction than elastomeric seals.

### Inflatable Seals for Critical Applications

For cleanrooms, biosafety laboratories, and pharmaceutical manufacturing where near-zero leakage is essential, inflatable seals provide bubble-tight shutoff. A pneumatic bladder inflates after the damper blades close, pressing a flexible membrane against the blade surface. These are complex, expensive, and require a compressed air supply and control system — but achieve leakage rates below 5 L/s/m² (1 CFM/ft²) at 1 kPa.

### Cold Climate Considerations

In climates where outdoor air dampers see sustained sub-zero temperatures, blade edge seals must be paired with thermal-break frame construction. Without a thermal break, condensation freezes on the frame, bonding the blade seal to the frame and causing actuator overload on the next open cycle. EPDM seals with silicone lubricant treatment and electric heat tracing on the frame can prevent ice lock-up in extreme conditions.

🔑 Key Takeaways

  • ✓EPDM rubber bulb seals are standard for general HVAC applications up to 95°C; silicone seals extend the range to 175°C where off-gassing is a concern.
  • ✓Stainless steel jamb seals are mandatory for smoke control dampers per UL 555S, maintaining integrity at 175°C for 30+ minutes.
  • ✓Metal seals demand higher actuator torque than elastomeric seals — always verify the actuator is sized for the installed seal type.
  • ✓Cold-climate outdoor air dampers need thermal-break frames and lubricated seals to prevent ice lock-up and actuator overload.
  • ✓Inflatable seals achieve bubble-tight shutoff for critical environments but add significant cost and complexity with compressed air systems.
