How Asian Building Codes Are Driving a Convergence in Energy Efficiency Specifications

Singapore's BCA Green Mark 2026, Hong Kong's BEAM Plus v2.0, and Dubai's Al Sa'fat now mandate submetering, continuous commissioning, and minimum 30% energy improvement — here is what it means for controls specifiers.

Share:XLinkedInTelegramWhatsApp

The conversation around building energy efficiency has shifted from "why should we?" to "by when?" Building codes across Asia — Singapore's BCA Green Mark 2026, Hong Kong's BEAM Plus v2.0, and Dubai's Al Sa'fat — have all updated within the 2025-2026 cycle to mandate submetering, continuous commissioning, and minimum 30% energy improvement over baseline. While each code approaches compliance differently, the specification implications are remarkably consistent.

Submetering Is Now Mandatory — Design It In from Day One

BCA Green Mark 2026 requires per-floor or per-zone energy submetering for new commercial buildings. BEAM Plus v2.0 extends this to major retrofits. For controls specifiers, this means BACnet-based energy meters must be designed into the BAS from the schematic phase — retrofitting submetering post-occupancy is 3-5x more expensive and often incompatible with proprietary BAS protocols.

Energy audits under ISO 50002 require interval data at 15-minute granularity. Proprietary BAS protocols that do not export at this granularity are increasingly non-compliant. BACnet/IP and Modbus TCP remain the safe specification choice across all three jurisdictions.

Continuous Commissioning Is Changing the Contract Model

Gulf projects increasingly include a 3-year monitoring-and-commissioning period in the controls tender. The specifier's deliverable shifts from "system commissioned at handover" to "system performing to design intent for 36 months." This fundamentally favours open-protocol BAS platforms that produce auditable trend logs at 15-minute intervals [ISO 50002].

The specifier's scope is changing: from "install and commission" to "install, commission, and monitor for 3 years." Proprietary BAS platforms that cannot export 15-minute interval data are dead on arrival in Gulf tenders.

IAQ and Energy Efficiency Are Converging — By Code

ASHRAE 90.1-2025 and EN 16798-1 both mandate demand-controlled ventilation tied to real-time occupancy data. The old fixed-outdoor-air-fraction approach satisfies neither code. CO₂-based DCV that maintains indoor air quality while reducing outdoor air treatment load by 30-40% is now the minimum standard [ASHRAE 62.1-2022].

The intersection of IAQ and energy efficiency is no longer a trade-off — it is a code requirement. Singapore's BCA Green Mark 2026 now mandates CO₂-based DCV in all new office builds. ASHRAE 90.1-2025 follows suit. Demand-controlled ventilation that responds to real-time occupancy saves 30-40% on outdoor air treatment costs while maintaining CO₂ below 800 ppm.

The Specification Language You Write Today

For consultants and specifiers working across markets, the message is clear: design for BACnet/IP or Modbus TCP, specify continuous monitoring capability, and budget for post-handover commissioning services. The codes are converging — and the specification language you write today determines whether your project is compliant in 2030.

The variance across these three codes is in enforcement timelines, not ambition. Net-zero ready by 2030-2035 is the common trajectory. The controls engineer who can deliver a single specification that satisfies all three jurisdictions — BACnet/IP, 15-minute interval data, CO₂-based DCV, and continuous commissioning capability — has a substantial competitive advantage.
